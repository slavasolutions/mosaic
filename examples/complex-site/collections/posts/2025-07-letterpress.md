# Why we still set type by hand

People ask why we keep a case of foundry type when polymer plates exist, when a laser cutter would do the linework in an afternoon, when we could send the file to a service in New Jersey and have proofs Tuesday. The honest answer is that it's slower in a way that catches mistakes.

When you set a line of metal type, you read it backwards while you set it, you read it forwards when you lock the form, you read it backwards again on the proof, and you read it forwards once more on the press sheet. Four reads, in four different orientations, in three different rooms. Errors don't survive that.

The other answer is that the letters have weight. Picking up an em quad and feeling the alloy in your hand is a small reminder that printing used to be a trade with a payroll. We like the reminder.

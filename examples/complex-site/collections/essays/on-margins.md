# In praise of margins

When students ask me what to look at first on a printed page, I tell them to look at the margins. Most of them think I'm being clever. I'm not. The margins are the part that gets the least attention in a draft and carries the most weight on the finished page.

## Why margins matter

A book's margins do four things at once. They give your hand somewhere to hold the page without covering the words. They give the eye somewhere to rest at the end of each line so it can find the next one. They give the binder room to trim without eating into the text. And they signal — quietly, before you've read a sentence — how much the maker cared.

A poetry chapbook with 12mm margins and a poetry chapbook with 22mm margins feel like different objects in the hand, even before you open them. The first feels economical, almost defensive. The second feels generous. Neither is wrong; they're saying different things.

## What changes between paper and screen

The interesting thing about reading on a phone is that the device imposes its own margins, and they're nearly always too narrow. The screen wants to fill itself. A book page, by contrast, has been argued over for five centuries by people who care about line length. The 65-character line is not arbitrary. Most apps ignore it.

## A practical note

If you set type and you can't decide on margins, do this: print three versions — narrow, medium, generous — and tape them to the wall for a day. Walk past them. The right one announces itself.
